Fact : CPR is a lifesaving technique useful in emergencies such as a heart attack or near drowning, where someone’s breathing or heartbeat has stopped.

Steps for rescue:

Check the Environment: Ensure the scene is safe for you and the victim. If the area is dangerous, move the person to a safer location if possible.
Check Responsiveness: Tap the person on the shoulder and shout, “Are you okay?” to see if they respond.
Call for Help: If the person is unresponsive, call emergency services immediately or ask someone else to call. If an Automated External Defibrillator (AED) is available, have someone retrieve it.

Position the Person: Lay the person flat on their back on a firm surface.
Head Tilt-Chin Lift: Place one hand on the person's forehead and gently tilt the head back. With your other hand, lift the chin forward to open the airway.

Look, Listen, and Feel: Look for chest movement, listen for breath sounds, and feel for air on your cheek for no more than 10 seconds.
If Not Breathing Normally: Begin CPR immediately if the person is not breathing or only gasping.

Hand Placement: Kneel beside the person. Place the heel of one hand in the center of the chest, between the nipples. Place your other hand on top of the first hand and interlock your fingers.
Compression Technique: Keep your elbows straight and position your shoulders directly above your hands. Use your body weight to compress the chest at least 2 inches (5 cm) deep for adults. For children, compress about 2 inches (5 cm), and for infants, compress about 1.5 inches (4 cm).
Compression Rate: Perform compressions at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute. Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions.

Head Tilt-Chin Lift: After 30 compressions, use the head-tilt-chin-lift technique again to open the airway.
Pinch the Nose: Pinch the person's nose shut with your fingers.
Give Two Breaths: Take a normal breath, place your mouth over the person's mouth, and create an airtight seal. Blow in to make the chest rise. Give two rescue breaths, each lasting about 1 second.
Continue CPR: If the chest does not rise, reposition the head and try again. If still unsuccessful, continue chest compressions.

Repeat: Continue the cycle of 30 chest compressions and two rescue breaths.
Do Not Stop: Continue CPR until emergency personnel arrive, an AED is ready to use, or the person shows signs of life (such as breathing or movement).

Turn on the AED: As soon as the AED arrives, turn it on and follow the voice prompts.
Attach Pads: Place the AED pads on the person’s bare chest, as shown in the AED diagrams.
Analyze: Allow the AED to analyze the heart’s rhythm. Ensure no one is touching the person during this time.
Shock Delivery: If the AED advises a shock, make sure no one is touching the person and press the shock button.
Continue CPR: After the shock is delivered, immediately resume CPR starting with chest compressions. Continue to follow AED prompts.

Step 8: Recovery Position (If the Person Starts Breathing)

Position: If the person starts breathing normally but remains unconscious, roll them onto their side into the recovery position.
Monitor: Continue to monitor their breathing until emergency help arrives.

Additional Notes:

Infants and Children: Adjust the depth and technique of compressions and breaths for infants and children, using one hand for children and two fingers for infants for chest compressions.
Continue Until Help Arrives: Never stop CPR until professional help takes over or the person starts to show signs of life.

FAQ's

Q. When should I perform CPR?

CPR should be performed when a person is unresponsive and not breathing or not breathing normally (e.g., only gasping). This situation indicates that their heart may have stopped, and immediate action is necessary.


Q. What if I'm not trained in CPR?

If you're not trained, perform hands-only CPR, which means doing chest compressions without rescue breaths. Push hard and fast in the center of the chest until emergency help arrives.


Q. Can I break someone's ribs during CPR?

It's possible to break ribs during CPR, especially in older adults. However, the risk of not performing CPR is much greater than the risk of broken ribs.


Q. Is CPR effective?

CPR can significantly increase the chances of survival for a person in cardiac arrest, especially if performed immediately. However, outcomes can vary depending on how quickly it is administered and the underlying cause of the cardiac arrest.


Q. What if I get tired during CPR?

If possible, alternate with another person every two minutes to avoid fatigue. If you're alone, do your best to keep going until help arrives.
